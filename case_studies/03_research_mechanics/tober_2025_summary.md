## 1. Summary

### Title and Authors
**Title:** *Alaskan Glacier Depths from a Decade of Airborne Radar Sounding*

**Authors:** B. S. Tober, M. S. Christoffersen, J. W. Holt, M. Truffer, and C. F. Larsen

**Status:** Non-peer-reviewed preprint submitted to EarthArXiv; manuscript submitted to *Journal of Geophysical Research: Earth Surface*

---

### Research Question / Objective
The study presents the first comprehensive analysis of NASA Operation IceBridge airborne radar sounding data collected over Alaskan and adjacent northwestern Canadian glaciers between 2013–2021. The primary objectives are to:
- Compile the most extensive inventory of ice thickness and bed elevation measurements for the Alaska-Yukon region to date
- Characterize subglacial topography, particularly overdeepenings and troughs
- Assess the limitations of radar sounding in temperate, high-relief mountain glacier environments
- Provide guidance for planning future airborne radar campaigns

---

### Methods Used
- **Radar systems:** Two long-wavelength airborne radar sounders — the University of Alaska Fairbanks High-Frequency system (UAFHF; 2013–2015) and the Arizona Radio Echo Sounder (ARES; 2015–2021) — with center frequencies of 2.5–5 MHz
- **Signal processing:** Pulse compression (matched filtering with boxcar window), coherent slow-time summing (factor of 100), rolling mean subtraction
- **Clutter simulation:** Off-nadir surface returns ("surface clutter") were modeled and compared to each radar profile to distinguish genuine bed returns from topographic artifacts
- **Manual picking:** Bed returns were digitized using the Radar Analysis Graphical Utility (RAGU); first-break picking for UAFHF impulse data, peak-amplitude picking for ARES pulse-compressed data
- **Surface elevation:** Derived from coincident lidar point clouds (median within first Fresnel zone); snow radar used as substitute where lidar was unavailable
- **Ice thickness conversion:** Englacial travel time converted to depth assuming a dielectric constant of 3.15 (wavespeed of 169 m µs⁻¹)
- **Bed elevation gridding:** Gaussian process regression over the Bering Glacier system at 100 m resolution, conditioned on radar measurements and ice-boundary surface elevations from the Copernicus-30 DEM
- **Uncertainty assessment:** Multi-interpreter comparison (N = 20 profiles per system) and crossover analysis at 1,829 profile intersections
- **Comparison:** Gridded radar results compared against global ice thickness models (Farinotti et al., 2019; Millan et al., 2022)

### Key Findings

| Finding | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Data coverage** | 5,600 linear-km of ice thickness and bed elevation measurements across 88 glaciers, from 31,700 linear-km of total radar profiles |
| **Bering Glacier subglacial trough** | Two prominent troughs beneath the piedmont lobe merge and extend ~55 km up-glacier to the Bagley Ice Valley; bed elevation remains below sea level throughout; likely a subglacial expression of the Bering Fault |
| **Maximum ice depth** | ~1,480 m of temperate ice measured in the Bagley Ice Valley — reportedly the greatest airborne temperate ice thickness measurement to date |
| **Overdeepened termini** | Many glacier termini (e.g., Kahiltna, Kennicott, Tweedsmuir, Taku, Bering, Nabesna) have retrograde/overdeepened beds, suggesting significant future proglacial lake expansion |
| **Below sea level grounding** | 35–40% of Bering Glacier (550–635 km²) is grounded below sea level |
| **Global model errors** | Mean absolute error of 240 m (Farinotti et al., 2019) and 145 m (Millan et al., 2022) compared to radar-gridded ice thickness for the Bering system |
| **Surface clutter challenge** | Off-nadir radar returns from valley walls remain the primary limitation for mapping valley glaciers; a formula is derived for optimal flight altitude as a function of valley width and expected ice thickness |
| **Measurement uncertainty** | Median crossover disagreement of 18 m in ice thickness and 15 m in bed elevation; wavespeed uncertainty contributes ~2% thickness error |

---

### Significance / Implications
- Provides the most comprehensive ice thickness inventory for Alaska-Yukon, directly reducing uncertainty in regional ice volume and sea level rise projections
- Reveals subglacial tectonic structures (Bering Fault, Bagley fault zone) hidden beneath ice cover
- Demonstrates that widespread overdeepening will drive proglacial lake expansion, with implications for glacial lake outburst flood hazards and downstream water resources
- Exposes significant errors in widely-used global ice thickness models for large Alaskan glaciers
- Offers practical, quantitative guidance (including a derived equation) for optimizing future airborne radar survey flight altitudes to minimize surface clutter interference
